## The Turing Test (1950)
* [cite_start]**Objective**: Proposed by Alan Turing to sidestep the question "Can a machine think?" by focusing on behavioral imitation[cite: 662, 663].
* [cite_start]**The Criterion**: A computer passes if a human interrogator cannot distinguish its responses from those of a human[cite: 664].
* **Required Capabilities**:
    * [cite_start]**Natural Language Processing (NLP)** [cite: 667]
    * [cite_start]**Knowledge Representation** [cite: 668]
    * [cite_start]**Automated Reasoning** [cite: 669]
    * [cite_start]**Machine Learning** [cite: 670]
* [cite_start]**Total Turing Test**: Adds requirements for **Computer Vision** and **Robotics**[cite: 671, 672, 674].

## Approaches to Intelligence
* [cite_start]**Thinking Rationally (Laws of Thought)**: Based on Aristotle's syllogisms and logicism[cite: 680, 681]. [cite_start]It uses formal notation for objects and relations but struggles with real-world uncertainty[cite: 682, 685].
* [cite_start]**Acting Rationally (Rational Agent Approach)**: An agent is something that acts to achieve the best outcome (or best expected outcome under uncertainty)[cite: 689, 690]. [cite_start]This approach is more general and scientifically amenable than pure imitation[cite: 691].

## Philosophical Foundations
* [cite_start]**Chinese Room Argument (John Searle, 1980)**: A thought experiment arguing that syntax (following rules) does not equal semantics (true understanding)[cite: 749, 751]. [cite_start]It challenges the claims of "Strong AI"[cite: 752].
* [cite_start]**Weak vs. Strong AI**: Weak AI focuses on specific tasks, while Strong AI aims for general, human-like intelligence.
* [cite_start]**Mind-Body Problem**: Investigates how physical computation relates to mental states (Dualism vs. Materialism)[cite: 695, 735].

## Limitations of Early Symbolic AI
* [cite_start]**Lack of Self-Learning**: Early systems relied on hand-coded rules and didn't improve over time[cite: 708].
* [cite_start]**Symbol Grounding Problem**: Difficulty connecting internal symbols to real-world meanings[cite: 710].
* [cite_start]**Combinatorial Explosion**: Early systems (1966-1973) failed on difficult problems due to the intractability of searching complex spaces[cite: 714, 715].
* **The "Problems"**:
    * [cite_start]**Frame Problem**: Determining what remains unchanged in a situation[cite: 766, 767].
    * [cite_start]**Qualification Problem**: Handling infinite possible exceptions to rules[cite: 768].
    * [cite_start]**Ramification Problem**: Dealing with the side effects of actions[cite: 769].

## AI History & Transitions
* **AI Winters**: 
    * [cite_start]**First Winter (1974-1980)**: Caused by funding cuts due to unmet expectations[cite: 728].
    * [cite_start]**Second Winter (1987-1993)**: Triggered by the collapse of the Lisp machine market[cite: 729].
* [cite_start]**Modern Shift**: The field moved from "weak methods" to domain-specific knowledge, probabilistic reasoning, and machine learning (Connectionism)[cite: 719, 720, 771].
